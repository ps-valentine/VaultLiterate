# Engaging Young People Who Use Drugs
A Harm Reduction Training Institute (HRTI) presentation by Alex Fillippelli (they/he) and Danielle Raghib (she/her).
## Learning Goals
1. Be able to identify and describe at least 3 challenges to engaging youth in harm reduction service provision.
2. Be able to explain 3 ways adolescent development and adultism influence service provider's interactions with you who use drugs.
3. Be able to demonstrate at least 2 adolescent development and cultural humility informed communication strategies to respond effectively in practice scenarios with youth who use drugs.
## Harm Reduction Best Practices for Overdose Prevention
- Education on overdose risks and warning signs.
- Access to naloxone and overdose response training.
- Honest, developmentally appropriate conversations about substance use.
- Safer use education without fear-based messaging.
- Supportive pathways to behavioral health and medical care.
- Family and community engagement when appropriate and safe.
## Why Youth-Focused Harm Reduction Matters
- Adolescents experience unique developmental, social, and environmental factors that influence substance use and overdose risk.
- Youth-centered harm reduction...
	- Meets young people where they are.
	- Prioritizes safety over punishment.
	- Builds trust and connection.
	- Reduces stigma and shame.
	- Increases the likelihood of help-seeking and engagement in care.
## Core Principles for Youth Conversations
- Effective harm reduction conversations are...
	- Nonjudgmental.
	- Collaborative.
	- Strengths-based.
	- Trauma-informed.
	- Developmentally responsive.
	- Culturally humble.
- Providers should...
	- Normalize honest discussion.
	- Avoid scare tactics.
	- Offer practical safety strategies.
	- Support incremental behavior change.
	- Meet youth where they are.
## Youth Engagement
- **Youth-Specific Considerations**
	- Teens may not identify as "users" or view their behavior as risky.
	- Peer influence and social belonging are major drivers of behavior.
	- Confidentiality is critical for trust-building; discussing privacy at the state of care helps build trust, as fear of parental or legal involvement is a major barrier to seeking help.
- **Providers should...**
	- Address social media influence and normalization of risk behaviors.
	- Online safety and exposure to misinformation.

### The Impact of Adultism and Power Imbalances
- **Adultism** occurs when young people are viewed as less capable, less credible, or less deserving of autonomy because of their age. Examples include...
	- Dismissing youth concerns or experiences.
	- Using punitive or controlling language.
	- Assuming resistance equals "noncompliance."
	- Excluding youth from decision-making.
- Youth engagement improves when providers...
	- Share decision-making power.
	- Offer choices whenever possible.
	- Validate emotions and lived experiences.
	- Communicate with transparency and respect.
### Understanding Adolescent Development
- Adolescents are still developing executive functioning, especially impulse control, emotional regulation, and perspective-taking.
- The prefrontal cortex (responsible for judgment and planning) develops later than the limbic system (responsible for emotion and reward), which can make youth more emotionally reactive, especially in online environments.
- Youth are neurologically wired to seek peer approval and belonging, and social media can amplify this drive.
### Developmentally Appropriate Engagement
- Adolescents may be...
	- Highly sensitive to judgment or criticism.
	- Focused on peer relationships and belonging.
	- ... (and more)
- Helpful approaches include...
	- Asking permission before giving advice.
	- Using open-ended questions.
	- Avoiding lectures or assumptions.
	- Keeping information clear and concrete.
	- Validating emotions and experiences.
### Open-Ended Questions That Build Trust
- Conversation Starters
	- What have you heard about fentanyl and overdose risks?
	- What helps you feel safe in social situations?
	- What concerns do you have about substance use?
	- What would safer use look like for you?
	- When you say... what do you mean?
