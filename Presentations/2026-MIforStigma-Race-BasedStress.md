#### What is complex trauma?
- Childhood abuse or neglect
- Domestic violence or unsafe relationships
- Sex trafficking or sexual exploitation
- Community violence or repeated threats
- Racism, discrimination, or chronic marginalization
- Homelessness, instability, or poverty-related survival stress
- Repeated loss, separation, or abandonment
- Living with addiction in the family or unsafe caregiving

#### Principles of CAMI
- Not being afraid to name race
- Cultural humility
- Be aware of your own biases
- Practitioner self-acceptance
#### How is CAMI different from MI?
- Doubles down on humanistic psychology by amplifying MI spirit using OARS
- Is an integrated self-affrimation/motivational interviewing approach (Sherman, 2013)
- Is a "stigma reduction intervention" in its focus on reducing stigma by communicating a "whole person" perspective
- CAMI minimizes defensiveness through affirmation
#### Cultural Humility as a CAMI Principle (Tervalon & Murray-García, 1998)
- Reflection: there is always something to learn from someone
- Respect: a duty to incorporate patient choices, preferences, and boundaries with care; emphasizing patient autonomy
	- Some cultures may not prioritize personal autonomy
	- Hierarchical relationships are the norm; they expect the provider to give them information

#### Traditional or collectivistic cultures
- People from traditional or collectivistic cultures may have different expectations of the provider
	- Collectivistic culture: the group is more important than the individual; based in hierarchical relationships; can assume the provider has their best interest at heart.
	- Causes confusion if asked, "what are your goals?"
#### Asking "what matters?"
- What is important to that person: their values and priorities
- A way to query cultural values
- Identify strengths
- Conveys "whole person" perspective
