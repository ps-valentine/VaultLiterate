## Preface
"Over the past four years, Kim Ganade, Edith Guilléwn Núñez, and Sal Núñez have engaged in ongoing discussions about the effects of colonialism on psychotherapy, with particular focus on Eurocentrism within academia and the behavioral health field.

In their conversations, they sought ways to foster discussions that would:
1. Encourage the development of psychotherapeutic practices rooted in cultural values.
2. Inspire clinicians to explore and integrate their clinical cultural identity into their practice.
3. Promote and normalize culturally affirming psychotherapy within BIPOC communities.
   
   Building on these reflections, they developed the Latinx Glossary, a resource aimed at gathering insights and tools to support psychotherapists working with Latinx communities. The project also seeks to empower clinicians by encouraging them to incorporate their own clinical cultural identity into their psychotherapeutic approach and normalizing their experience as culturally affirming providers."

#### Prompts/Questions for Panelists
- Please discuss and provide examples of how the key Latinx cultural values are embedded in your clinical approach.
	- Showing-up as a full human despite being a clinician (*personalismo*).
		- Introducing himself, where your people are from, what they should know about you...
	- And, inversely, acknowledge those you're working with as full humans, not just patients.
		- Where are your people from? Who is your community? What should I know about you?
		- De dónde es usted? Que están molestando hoy?
		- Welcome to my office. Would you like some tea or a snack?
	- Being respectful (and maybe formal) (*respeto*).
		- Use honorifics, such as "Don", "Señora", etc.
		- Discussion of "using *tú* vs. *usted*?" Connotations around age.
	- Acknowledge the difficulties of accessing medical care, *utilizing* medical care (in the face of stigma), and the reality of our current world.
	- Acknowledge, if you are not from the same culture as your patient, that you have respect for those differences. Acknowledge your differences, but also your similarities, and explain *how* you intend to bridge that gap.
		- Less clinical, more of a guide.
		- Acknowledging differences can build connection.
	- *Familismo* acknowledges the family as a unit, not a group of individuals.
	- Family is a source of strength for some, and stress for others, but it is often a central topic (in therapy).
	- Extending the concept of *familismo* to chosen family, close friends, etc.
	- *Pocho* and Mexican-American slang.
- How do you use self-disclosure, authenticity, and traditional and community-based practices in your approach?
	- Come as your authentic self.
		- e.g., one presenter talks with her hands, has candles (even though they can't be used in her workplace) and uses sage spray to scent her workplace, etc.
		- Artwork, important spiritual artifacts, etc.
	- "We have to self-disclose because we're human."
	- Discussion around how to breakdown the top-down hierarchical nature of therapy. Use of self-disclosure as a method to break-down hierarchy.
	- Use self-disclosure in ways that don't solicit assistance from the patient (and considering that patient's tendencies; e.g., are they a caring person who often tries to help others?).
		- A story including self-disclosure as a way to connect.
		- Bringing food to connect.
	- Inclusion of family in sessions.
	- Working as a team as a community-based practice. Extending the 'working as a team' concept beyond your work team, to other organizations, businesses, etc.
	- *Plática* vs. *desahogo*.
	- "*Lo siento*" not as "I'm sorry" but as an "I hear you and how you tell me that's impacting you."
	- Especially around grief: "When you were growing up, how was that tended to in your community?" "How did your family do this?"
- How can culturally grounded healing practices co-exist within western mental health frameworks and what barriers often arise?
	- (At Spanish language group therapy with Latinx clients. ) "There's always a PowerPoint in the background, but we never use it."
	- Utilizing metaphors: soup/cooking, soccer, monsters, trees, etc...
	- *Dichos*!
	- Humor as coping.
	- Validating folks in their identities, heritages, coping mechanisms, etc.
	- Connecting patients with *curanderas* who might have access to a *temascal* or who can provide a *limpia*, and other culturally-responsive resources.
