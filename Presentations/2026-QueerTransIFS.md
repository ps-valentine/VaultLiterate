## Training Goals
At the end of this training, you will be able to...
1. Integrate into practice the history and basic assumptions of the Internal Family Systems (IFS) therapy model regarding its non-pathological view of the mind's multiplicity and the concept of Self.
2. Apply three different kinds of parts in the IFS model of multiplicity.
3. Practice identifying differences in the role of the therapist in the IFS model compared to other models of therapy and apply these differences when viewing the client's internal system and its role(s) in mental health.
## IFS in a Nutshell
- IFS stands for Internal Family Systems.
	- A framework for understanding the complexity of human experience.
	- A healing approach - with promising outcomes in treating anxiety, depression, and trauma.
- White supremacy tells us that there is one appropriate way to show-up, at all times.
- IFS provides a lens that allows for a greater recognition of who we all are.
- It *does* use a lot of jargon.
## Origins of IFS
- Richard Schwartz: eating disorder treatment, family therapy
- Patients talked about parts
- Influenced by Gestalt, Hakomi, Core Shamanism
- Tried to work with parts and get to know them instead of control them
- Self as key feature of IFS
## IFS Basic Assumptions
- Multiplicity (*not* as in someone with multiple personalities): we all have Self and parts
- Parts are forced into roles
- Parts have positive intentions for us
- Our parts interact with each other and with other people's parts/systems
## Cultural Acknowledgment
- IFS did not invent parts or multiplicity; this is part of many different cultures' views of human experience, including Native American cultures.
- Like many therapy approaches, there are known and unknown influences, sometimes without due credit. We are continually trying to gather and update our information to accurately attribute the source of these practices.
## Goal of IFS
- Unblend and differentiate from parts
- Allow parts to trust us (allow us to lead / be Self-led)
- Liberate parts from extreme roles
- Balance & harmony inside
	- "Internal secure attachment"
- Bring Self energy to external systems - possibility here for systems change and collective liberation
## The Internal System
### Self / Self Energy
- There is the experience of ourselves that is not a part.
- Self can be described as...
	- Our inner essence or core wisdom
	- "The you-est you"
	- Seat of consciousness
- When we are not **blended** with parts, we can be Self-led - honoring our deepest truths and needs.
### Characteristics of Self
- Clarity
- Connectedness
- Calm
- Curiosity
- Creativity
- Courage
- Confidence
- Compassion
- also see choice, consent, collectivism...
### Types of Parts
- Protectors
	- **Managers:** pro-active, day to day, often praised or socially acceptable
	- **Firefighters:** reactive, can be extreme or urgent, not thinking about long term or relationships
	- Use different strategies to protect us from overwhelm or pain
- **Exiles:** parts hidden away that are carrying core wounds, typically vulnerable or young
#### Managers
- **Managers:** pro-active, day to day, often praised or socially acceptable
	- Pro-active / pre-emptive
	- Keep us feeling safe, secure, and in control
	- Often try to control, risk-averse
	- Manage day to day life, rules to live by, "shoulds"
	- Protect against anything that leads to vulnerability, pain, and instability
	- Work to prevent the activation of exiles
- Common Manager Strategies
	- Do it all!
	- Don't complain, make it seem like you're keeping it together
	- Criticize (yourself or others)
	- Micromanage or control
	- Be exceptional
	- Perfectionism or achievement orientation
	- Self-sacrificing or people-pleasing
	- Caretaking
	- Worrying
	- Passive-aggressiveness
- Common Manager Strategies in Queer Populations (per observation of presentation attendees)
	- Overachieving to secure social currency (queer exceptionalism)
	- Focusing on fitting in
	- Being really good at caring for others / focusing on the feelings of others to feel safe
	- Presenting in a way that makes others comfortable, passing, blending-in
	- Compartmentalization
	- Intolerance of difference within queer communities
	- OCD and/or eating disorders to feel in control
	- Focusing on accomplishments to assuage internalized poor self image or external judgment re: acceptability (queer exceptionalism)
#### Firefighters
- **Firefighters:** reactive, can be extreme or urgent, not thinking about long term or relationships
	- Reactive
	- Extreme, may seem "out of control"
	- Numb, douse, or soothe emotions
	- Distract
	- Release pressure
	- Will do whatever it takes to get away from exiles' pain
	- Not concerned about consequences or relationships
- Common Firefighter Strategies
	- Reac
	- Explode
	- Check out with substances
	- Doom scroll
	- Addictive / compulsive behaviors (including socially acceptable ones)
	- Rage
	- Self-harm
	- Over/undersleeping
	- Can seem dangerous or extreme, but they feel it is justified - there's no other way.
- Common Firefighter Strategies in Queer Populations (per observation of presentation attendees)
	- Substance use
	- Tattoos, piercings, haircuts (as a distraction)
	- Unsafe sex
	- Frequent cheating or serial dating / cutting off relationships abruptly
	- Negative social behaviors to affirm belonging to identity / social groups
	- Shutdown or collapse, dissociation
	- Co-dependency
	- People-pleasing, fawning
	- Self-harm, suicidality

### IFS Parts Work
- Respecting the protective system is how we foster change.
- Slow is fast, fast is slow.
- This is not easy in grind culture of when we face constant urgency.
- The most effective path to being skilled as an IFS therapist is getting to know your own system of parts. It comes from heart-led, embodied experience, not from the head.
### Gender & IFS in Practice
- Different parts of the same person can have different genders or gender expressions: this may be true for people who are not trans and/or nonbinary.
- Never assume a part's gender or pronouns.
- Follow the client's lead; don't use pronouns and/or gender language unless/until the client does. Refer to it as "the part" or "it" or "the one that..."
- Avoid referring to younger parts as "little boys" or "little girls" if not explicitly named by the client.
### Internal Dynamics for Queer/Trans Clients
- Some parts may have more concerns about gender/sexuality.
- Some more about one's own felt/bodily experience.
- Some parts may be more concerned with external perceptions, how the client will be treated, and safety.
- Many trans and/or nonbinary clients have young parts that react to seeing affirmed/present-day version of client's self.
- Some parts may be polarized with others...
	- About authenticity versus safety

### Guiding Principles / Takeaways
- Be aware of your own gender/sexuality and parts that may have reactions to others' gender/sexuality.
- Educate yourself about queer/trans experience, culture and history, but remain curious about your clients' individual experiences and histories.
- Do not assume the gender/pronouns of any part! Not all parts have a gender. Some parts could be nonbinary.
- Do note assume that gender is one singular part!
- Refrain from assuming pronouns of younger parts based on person's current gender identity/presentation.
