# Implementation of a Pelvic Floor Physical Therapy Program for Transgender Women Undergoing Gender-Affirming Vaginoplasty
## Abstract
### Objective
"To describe the incidence of pelvic floor dysfunction in transgender women undergoing gender-affirming vaginoplasty and outcomes in a program providing pelvic floor physical therapy (PT)."
### Methods
"We conducted a retrospective, single-institution study on vaginoplasty patients between May 1, 2016, and February 28, 2018; all were referred for pelvic floor PT. We reviewed medical records for baseline demographics, medical comorbidities, prior surgeries, insurance data, attendance at pelvic floor PT, and dilation success at 3 and 12 months."
### Results
"Seventy-two of 77 patients (94%) attended pelvic floor PT at least once. **Preoperative pelvic floor PT identified a high incidence of potential problems**: 42% had pelvic floor dysfunction, 37% had bowel dysfunction. Of those patients found to have dysfunction preoperatively, the rate of resolution by the first postoperative visit of pelvic floor and bowel dysfunction were 69% and 73%, respectively. **There were significantly lower rates of pelvic floor dysfunction postoperatively for those patients who attended pelvic floor PT both preoperatively and postoperatively compared with only postoperatively** (28% vs 86%, P=.006). Patients reporting a history of abuse had a significantly higher rate of preoperative pelvic floor muscle dysfunction (91% vs 31%, P<.001). Successful dilation at 3 months in all patients was 89%."
### Conclusion
"Pelvic floor physical therapists identify and help patients resolve pelvic floor-related problems before and after surgery. We find strong support for pelvic floor PT for patients undergoing gender-affirming vaginoplasty."
## Introduction
- "Vaginoplasty is a gender-affirming genital surgery for transgender women. **The goals of vaginoplasty are to create a natural-appearing vulva with a correctly positioned urethra, a sensate clitoris constructed from the glans penis, and a vaginal canal for receptive intercourse.** Satisfaction rates are high for transgender women who undergo vaginoplasty."
- "Surgical disruption of the pelvic floor can disrupt support for the urethral sphincter, and it divides parts of the puborectalis muscle (part of the levator ani complex), which may affect rectal function and continence. Stress urinary incontinence after vaginoplasty has been reported as 16–33%."
- "To avoid stenosis of the neovaginal canal by the process of scar contracture after surgery, patients must perform **dilation** of the neovagina postoperatively. Although most people have little awareness or understanding of the anatomy or function of their pelvic floor muscles, neovaginal dilation requires direct contact and interaction with the altered anatomy shortly after a major surgery. Poor healing after surgery can lead to persistent pelvic pain or difficulty with dilation or sexual activity."
- "The primary goal of **preoperative pelvic floor PT** consultation is to identify any functional limitation or disability. Therapists first ask questions to determine baseline voiding and defecation patterns, then perform a pelvic floor muscle exam with specific focus on pelvic floor muscle tone and coordinated muscle contraction and relaxation."
- "Postoperatively, the therapist supports them as they perform dilation by examining for and reducing overactive pelvic floor muscles."
## Methods
- "This is a retrospective case series from a single institution. Since we began offering gender-affirming vaginoplasty in May 2016, each person who is scheduled for vaginoplasty is referred to pelvic floor PT before surgery and after they start dilation (which routinely commences at day 10 postoperatively), for at least two scheduled consultations. The purpose of pelvic floor PT is explained to the patient orally and with a written handout at the time of surgical consultation."
- "...patients who live locally sometimes have a consultation well in advance of their surgery, especially if they are thought by the surgical team to have special need for pelvic floor PT. Patients coming from afar usually have their preoperative consultation in the week before surgery. Patients who wish to continue to follow-up with PT further are able to do so, and patients who are identified by the therapists as having difficulty with pelvic floor muscle control are encouraged to attend further PT appointments."
- "As it is common for transgender people to be adverse to receiving health care owing to previous poor experiences in the health care setting, it was important in our program that the patient was educated in the rationale for involving pelvic floor PT, by the surgical team and by the physical therapist. It was equally important to us that the patient was empowered to choose whether to pursue additional preoperative PT or postoperative PT. As transgender people seeking medical and surgical transition intervention already have multiple requirements of genital hair removal and mental health visits and letters, we did not want this PT examination and treatment to seem like a barrier or obstacle to them receiving their gender-affirming care."
- "If any impairment in relaxation was found, the patient was taught to inhale slowly to expand their lower abdomen and pelvic floor with verbal, visual, and manual cues. They were also taught two to three exercises to externally stretch the levator ani complex. The exercises were **double knee to chest stretch and a child's pose or a full deep squat** to stretch the perineal area. If the person was found to be physically strong, they were taught a supported deep squat to promote extensibility in the pelvic region."
- "Pelvic floor PT provides additional individualized care and treatment depending on the dysfunction. Nonrelaxing, high tone, or overactive muscle may have the accompanying complaints of slowed urinary stream, constipation, straining to defecate, or pain are generally addressed with relaxation strategies of deep abdominal breathing, of positioning in poses of neutral hip rotation with deep hip flexion to provide and externally generated stretch on the pelvic floor muscle and this may include strategies for managing constipation by improving the defecation angle or improving urinary flow and continence mechanisms and methods for alleviating postoperative pain with the introduction of a perineal cushion and help identify tools that would be helpful for each individual..."
- "Physical therapists may also provide desensitization techniques for muscular and postsurgical pain and identify activities that help down-regulate the central nervous system for the specific patient (eg, breathing exercises, guided relaxation). Another focus of the pelvic floor PT includes guidance and exercise prescription for return to normal daily activities, work, and resuming exercise."
- "We retrospectively collected data on the success of dilation. At their first outpatient surgical follow-up visit 10–14 days after vaginoplasty, a member of the surgical team staff instructs and assists patients in performing dilation of the neovagina. We provide patients with a set of dilators (www.soulsource.com): size P2, #1, #2, and #3, with a smaller P1 or larger #4 dilator available on request (Table 1). Intraoperatively, a custom mold (11 cm in circumference; 3.5 cm in diameter) is used to create the neovagina, equivalent to a #3 dilator. Patients are instructed to perform dilation three times per day for 30 minutes each session. They are instructed on how to graduate to successive sizes until they reach their personal goal size dilator."

## Discussion
- "Physical therapists evaluating and educating patients preoperatively is not a novel concept. Physical therapists are often consulted before surgery for patients planned for knee or hip replacement, spinal surgery, bariatric surgery, hysterectomy, and planned cesarean deliveries. Physical therapy applied preoperatively or postoperatively is associated with significant improvement in functional outcomes and quality of life in patients undergoing orthopedic or cardiac surgeries."
- "Preoperative pelvic floor PT is able to identify areas of concerns that could potentially hinder postoperative recovery. Indeed, pelvic floor PT identified a high rate of preoperative pelvic floor muscle (42%) and bowel dysfunction (37%) in transgender women, which is higher than reported in the cisgender female population. A 2010 review reported the rate of pelvic floor dysfunction among cisgender females at 25%, including 17% of women with moderate-to-severe urinary incontinence, 9% with fecal incontinence. We found patients who reported a history of abuse had significantly higher rates of pelvic muscle dysfunction preoperatively (91% vs 31%). The rate of reported lifetime sexual assault in the transgender population is 47%16 compared with 21% in the cisgender population. In the cisgender population, urinary, bowel, sexual, and pelvic floor dysfunction have well-known associations with prior physical, sexual, or emotional abuse."
- "... the practice of “tucking” the penis and scrotum, avoidance of public bathrooms, medications with antiandrogens and estrogen, as well as the high incidence of abuse in this population could all contribute to abnormal pelvic floor."
- "Physical therapists educate patients about the function and anatomy of the pelvic floor and provide practical advice about dilation, such as body positioning to facilitate pelvic muscle relaxation and opening of the bony pelvic outlet by encouraging lying supine with anterior pelvic tilt, allowing sacral flexion and coccyx extension and increasing the distance between the coccyx and pubic arch to help compensate for the narrow subpubic angle of the testosterone-influenced pelvis. Additionally, the physical therapists teach patients exercises they can do at home in the lead up to surgery. The home program includes breathing exercises with coordinated pelvic floor contraction, and lumbopelvic and hip stretches that assist with stretching and lengthening of the muscles around the pelvic girdle to assist in achieving the necessary positions for active insertion of the dilator postoperatively."
- "We report a high prevalence of multiple types of pelvic floor dysfunction in this population; thus, it is our strong belief that patients undergoing gender-affirming vaginoplasty benefit from seeing pelvic floor PT preoperatively and postoperatively. Recently, another program has published similar high incidence of pelvic floor dysfunction in this population."
